Quantum foam≲10⁻¹⁶ m

Below this depth, distance itself gets blurry. The scrollbar could keep going down another nineteen decades to the Planck length (1.6×10⁻³⁵ m) — but nothing measurable lives there, so this is where the journey starts.

The proton0.84 fm

Three quarks, bound by gluons that flicker in and out of existence. A proton's charge radius is 0.84 femtometers, and 99.9% of your mass is this glue, not the quarks themselves.

The nucleus~10⁻¹⁴ m

A gold nucleus — 79 protons, 118 neutrons — is about 14 fm across. Everything around it, for the next four decades of your scroll, is empty.

The atom~1 Å

You crossed four powers of ten of vacuum to get here. Blow a hydrogen atom up to the size of a stadium and its nucleus is a pea at midfield. The cloud you see is probability, not stuff.

The double helix2 nm wide

DNA is two nanometers across, but unspooled, the copy in each of your cells runs about two meters. You carry roughly 40 trillion copies of this molecule.

Below light~100 nm

A virus is around 100 nanometers — smaller than the wavelengths of visible light (380–700 nm). Everything you have seen so far is, strictly speaking, invisible: no microscope using light can ever show it.

The cell~10 µm

An E. coli is 2 µm; a red blood cell 7.8 µm. At this scale water feels like honey and swimming means corkscrewing — inertia stops mattering.

The pixel~10⁻⁴ m

A human hair is ~70 µm. On a typical laptop panel, one pixel is about a tenth of a millimeter — you just scrolled past the grain of the very screen you're reading. Each one is three colored lamps pretending to be white.

The city~10⁴ m

Ten kilometers frames a city. Manhattan's grid blocks are ~80 m apart; Central Park is 4 km end to end. This is the largest scale a human can walk across in an afternoon.

The planet1.27×10⁷ m

Earth, 12,756 km across. Every person who has ever lived — around 117 billion of us — fits inside this frame. The International Space Station orbits a mere 400 km up: at this zoom, its altitude is a few pixels.

The star1.39×10⁹ m

The Sun is 1.39 million kilometers across — wide enough that the Moon's entire orbit around Earth would fit inside it, with room to spare.

One AU1.496×10¹¹ m

The Earth–Sun distance: one astronomical unit. Light — the fastest thing permitted — needs 8 minutes 19 seconds to cross it. Check the readout: the light-time of your viewport is now measured in minutes.

The system~10¹³ m

Voyager 1, launched 1977, is the farthest human-made object: roughly 25 billion kilometers out. After half a century of falling through space, it is still inside this single frame.

The neighborhood~10¹⁶ m

A light-year is 9.46×10¹⁵ m. The nearest star, Proxima Centauri, sits 4.2 light-years away — 40 trillion kilometers of nothing between us and the next hydrogen furnace.

The galaxy~10²¹ m

The Milky Way: 100,000 light-years, a few hundred billion stars, all orbiting a black hole of 4.3 million solar masses. The Sun's lap takes 230 million years — it has gone around about 20 times, ever.

The Local Group~10²³ m

Andromeda, 2.5 million light-years away, is approaching at 110 km/s. In four billion years the two spirals merge. From here on, galaxies are the dust.

The web~10²⁵ m

Matter settles into filaments strung between voids — the largest structure there is. Our supercluster, Laniakea, spans 520 million light-years and holds a hundred thousand galaxies, and it is one thread among millions.

The horizon8.8×10²⁶ m

The observable universe: 93 billion light-years across, holding hundreds of billions of galaxies. Not the edge of everything — just the edge of what light has had time to bring us.

You are here

10⁰ — one meter. A chair, a dog, an open laptop, you. Of the forty-three scales on this page, this is the only one rendered in HTML — because it is the only scale where browsers, and their users, exist.
