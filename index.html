Quantum foam≲10⁻¹⁶ m

Below this depth, distance itself gets blurry. The scrollbar could keep going down another nineteen decades to the Planck length (1.6×10⁻³⁵ m) — but nothing measurable lives there, so this is where the journey starts.

The proton0.84 fm

Three quarks, bound by gluons that flicker in and out of existence. A proton's charge radius is 0.84 femtometers, and 99.9% of your mass is this glue, not the quarks themselves.

The nucleus~10⁻¹⁴ m

A gold nucleus — 79 protons, 118 neutrons — is about 14 fm across. Everything around it, for the next four decades of your scroll, is empty.

The atom~1 Å

You crossed four powers of ten of vacuum to get here. Blow a hydrogen atom up to the size of a stadium and its nucleus is a pea at midfield. The cloud you see is probability, not stuff.

The double helix2 nm wide

DNA is two nanometers across, but unspooled, the copy in each of your cells runs about two meters. You carry roughly 40 trillion copies of this molecule.

Below light~100 nm

A virus is around 100 nanometers — smaller than the wavelengths of visible light (380–700 nm). Everything you have seen so far is, strictly speaking, invisible: no microscope using light can ever show it.

The cell~10 µm

An E. coli is 2 µm; a red blood cell 7.8 µm. At this scale water feels like honey and swimming means corkscrewing — inertia stops mattering.

The pixel~10⁻⁴ m

A human hair is ~70 µm. On a typical laptop panel, one pixel is about a tenth of a millimeter — you just scrolled past the grain of the very screen you're reading. Each one is three colored lamps pretending to be white.

The city~10⁴ m

Ten kilometers frames a city — Paris inside the Périphérique is about that wide. From here the human world reads as texture: rooftops, field boundaries, and the river that explains why the town was built exactly there. This is the largest scale a person can walk across in an afternoon.

The planet1.27×10⁷ m

Earth, 12,756 km across. Every person who has ever lived — around 117 billion of us — fits inside this frame. The International Space Station orbits a mere 400 km up: at this zoom, its altitude is a few pixels.

The star1.39×10⁹ m

The Sun is 1.39 million kilometers across — wide enough that the Moon's entire orbit around Earth would fit inside it, with room to spare.

One AU1.496×10¹¹ m

The Earth–Sun distance: one astronomical unit. Light — the fastest thing permitted — needs 8 minutes 19 seconds to cross it. Check the readout: the light-time of your viewport is now measured in minutes.

The system~10¹³ m

Voyager 1, launched 1977, is the farthest human-made object: roughly 25 billion kilometers out. After half a century of falling through space, it is still inside this single frame.

The neighborhood~10¹⁶ m

A light-year is 9.46×10¹⁵ m. The nearest star, Proxima Centauri, sits 4.2 light-years away — 40 trillion kilometers of nothing between us and the next hydrogen furnace.

The galaxy~10²¹ m

The Milky Way: 100,000 light-years, a few hundred billion stars, all orbiting a black hole of 4.3 million solar masses. The Sun's lap takes 230 million years — it has gone around about 20 times, ever.

The Local Group~10²³ m

Andromeda, 2.5 million light-years away, is approaching at 110 km/s. In four billion years the two spirals merge. From here on, galaxies are the dust.

The web~10²⁵ m

Matter settles into filaments strung between voids — the largest structure there is. Our supercluster, Laniakea, spans 520 million light-years and holds a hundred thousand galaxies, and it is one thread among millions.

The horizon8.8×10²⁶ m

The observable universe: 93 billion light-years across, holding hundreds of billions of galaxies. Not the edge of everything — just the edge of what light has had time to bring us.

Electroweak erat ≈ 10⁻¹¹ s

The universe is a trillionth of a second old and hotter than 10¹⁵ K. The four forces have just finished separating; everything that exists is a plasma of quarks, gluons and light.

Confinementt ≈ 10 µs

Cooling below two trillion kelvin, quarks lock together three by three. The first protons and neutrons appear — nothing since has ever been hot enough to unbind them.

One secondt + 1 s

Neutrinos stop interacting and fly free. They are still passing through you — about 60 billion per square centimeter per second, almost unchanged since this moment.

Three minutest + 180 s

In about a quarter of an hour the universe forges the hydrogen and helium of everything that will ever exist: three quarters, one quarter. The recipe has not changed since.

Matter takes overt + 50,000 yr

Until here, light outweighed matter. From this crossover on, matter rules gravity and can begin, slowly, to clump.

First lightt + 380,000 yr

The fog of electrons clears; photons fly free. That first light is still everywhere — stretched a thousandfold into microwaves, the oldest thing you can observe.

The dark agest + ~40 Myr

For tens of millions of years there is not a single star. The universe is a slowly cooling cloud of neutral hydrogen — completely dark, everywhere.

First starst + ~300 Myr

The first stars are monsters, a hundred solar masses, dead within a few million years. Their ashes are the first elements heavier than lithium — every atom of carbon in your body included.

Young galaxiest + 1 Gyr

Gravity has braided matter into filaments; galaxies light up at the crossings. Reionization completes — the universe becomes transparent for good.

The Sun ignitest + 9.2 Gyr

In a recycled cloud enriched by generations of dead stars, a disk collapses: the Sun ignites and planets accrete from the leftovers, in barely 100 million years.

Lifet + 10 Gyr

Barely 200 million years after the oceans condense, chemistry starts copying itself. It has not stopped since — you are the current draft.

Living memorya few weeks ago

Ten kilometers of land, seen from above. Everything below this line is photographable; everything past it is inference. From here the page runs backwards — every 900 pixels takes you ten times further into the past, and the camera never moves.

The great acceleration~50 years ago

Half of everything humans have ever built, they built after this line. More than half of all fossil carbon ever burned was burned after it too. The map changes faster here than in the previous thousand years.

The works~200 years ago

Brick, coal and the first dead-straight lines the landscape had ever seen: the railway obeys the engine, not the terrain. The town swells around the works, and for the first time in history the air above a valley turns brown.

The walled town~800 years ago

A ring of stone, and inside it perhaps four thousand people. The fields radiate outward in strips, each household working a scatter of them — no hedges, no fences. The cathedral takes three generations to build: nobody who laid its first stone ever saw it finished.

Rome~2,000 years ago

Surveyors cut the countryside into a grid of squares 710 meters on a side, and handed one to each veteran. Look at the modern fields two decades up this page: some of those hedges still follow the Roman lines.

The first cities~5,500 years ago

Enough grain in one place that someone has to count it — and writing is invented to keep the accounts. Literature arrives centuries later. The first thing ever written down was a receipt.

The first fields~11,000 years ago

The ice has just gone. Within a few thousand years, in a handful of places at once, people stop following the food and start planting it. Everything on this page below this line — every city, every empire — sits on top of this one habit.

The ice~40,000 years ago

A glacier fills the valley and the river runs under it. Somewhere in the rock nearby, someone presses a hand to a wall and blows ochre around it. Those hands are still there. They are the oldest thing a person ever left saying: I was here.

Our species~200,000 years ago

Anatomically us, and nobody here. Everything you know how to do was learned after this line, but the people at it had exactly your brain — they simply hadn't been told anything yet.

Fire~1 million years ago

The only human mark in this whole valley is a single point of light. Cooking makes food cheap to digest, guts shrink, brains grow. Every other page of this history is downstream of that one flame.

Before anyone10 million years ago

No hominin has stood here. No one will for another eight million years. The valley simply is — the state it has been in for 99.9% of its existence.

The impact66 million years ago

A rock the size of a city arrives at 20 km/s. Within hours the sky over this valley is raining glass and burning. Three quarters of all species end here — and the survivors' descendants are reading this.

The dinosaurs250 million years ago

They hold this valley for 165 million years. Anatomically modern humans have held it for 0.2% as long. Stegosaurus is further in time from Tyrannosaurus than Tyrannosaurus is from you.

Only cells1 billion years ago

Life has been here for three billion years and has invented nothing you would notice: no leaf, no eye, no bone. For seven eighths of life's history, the most complicated thing alive was a single cell.

No ground yet4.5 billion years ago

There is no valley, no river, no rock to stand on — only a molten surface still being hit. Everything this page has shown you happened on the cooled skin of this.

You are here

10⁰ — one meter. A chair, a dog, an open laptop, you. Of the forty-three scales on this page, this is the only one rendered in HTML — because it is the only scale where browsers, and their users, exist.
